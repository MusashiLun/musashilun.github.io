Uncover the deep history of life on Earth

See how ancient life continues to shape our understanding of biology today.

Figure 1 (Left) Annotated replica of Paradoxides davidis fossil specimen, carefully highlighting key anatomical features including the cephalon, thorax, and pygidium for educational and comparative anatomy purposes (Lun, 2025).
Figure 2 (Right) The image shows a remarkably well-preserved fossil of Paradoxides gracilis, with detailed anatomical labeling, visible segmentation, and notable preservation of fine structural details. Fossil housed at the American Museum of Natural History.
(‘Paradoxides’, 2005; Lun, 2025)

Figure 3 Comparative fossil illustrations by Robert Hooke and intaglio engravings from Posthumous Works (1705).
(a) Hooke’s original drawing of 'snake-stones' (ammonites), showing variations in form, cross-sections, and sutures to support their organic origin. (British Library, Ms Add. 5262, no. 152.)
(b) Corresponding engraving from Posthumous Works (1705), Tab: Ia, closely based on Hooke’s drawing. Includes ammonites, internal features, and comparative shells like nautilus and echinoids.

Figure 4 (Left) The geologic time scale, shown as a log-spiral with major events in Earth's history. A megaannus (Ma) represents one million (106) years.
Figure 5 (Right) Evolutionary Tree of Life. This diagram shows how major life forms evolved from a common ancestor. Early life and microbes appear at the bottom, while invertebrates, plants, and fungi branch out in the middle. Vertebrates, including mammals and birds, are at the top. The image highlights evolution’s branching path and life’s diversity.
